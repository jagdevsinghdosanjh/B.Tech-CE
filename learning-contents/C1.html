üìò Chapter 1: Introduction to Matrices

A matrix is a rectangular arrangement of numbers into rows and columns, used to represent systems, transformations, and structured data.

üßÆ Notation

A matrix \( A \) of size \( m \times n \) is written as:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & \dots & a_{1n} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \dots & a_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \]

üìö Types of Matrices

TypeDescriptionExample
Row MatrixOne row\( \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \)
Column MatrixOne column\( \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 8 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} \)
Zero MatrixAll entries zero\( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \)
Square MatrixRows = Columns\( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \)
Diagonal MatrixOnly diagonal elements are non-zero\( \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \)
Scalar MatrixDiagonal values are equal\( \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \)
Identity MatrixDiagonal = 1\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)
Upper TriangularZeros below diagonal\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \)
Lower TriangularZeros above diagonal\( \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \)
üí° Did You Know? Every identity matrix is also scalar, diagonal, square, and symmetric!

üìù Practice Problem

Q: What type of matrix is \[ M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}? \]

Answer: This is a Scalar Matrix of order \( 3 \times 3 \).
