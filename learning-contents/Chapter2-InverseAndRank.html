📘 Chapter 2: Inverse and Rank of a Matrix

🔁 Inverse of a Matrix

  • The inverse of a matrix \( A \), denoted \( A^{-1} \), exists only if \( A \) is a square and nonsingular matrix.
  • If \( A^{-1} \) exists, then: \[ A \cdot A^{-1} = A^{-1} \cdot A = I \]
  • You can compute the inverse using:
    • Adjoint method: \( A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \cdot \text{adj}(A) \)
    • Row-reduction (Gauss–Jordan elimination)

Example:

Given \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \det(A) = (1)(4) - (2)(3) = -2 \] Using adjoint method: \[ \text{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad A^{-1} = \frac{1}{-2} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

📏 Rank of a Matrix

  • Rank is the maximum number of linearly independent rows or columns.
  • Denoted as \( \text{rank}(A) \), it's the dimension of the row space (or column space).
  • Used to determine:
    • Solvability of systems \( Ax = b \)
    • Whether inverse exists (\( \text{rank} = n \))

Example:

Let \[ B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Row 2} = 2 \cdot \text{Row 1} \Rightarrow \text{rank}(B) = 1 \]

🧠 Key Takeaways

  • \( \text{If } \det(A) = 0 \Rightarrow A^{-1} \text{ does not exist} \)
  • A matrix has an inverse ⇔ full rank ⇔ non-zero determinant
  • Rank reveals how much information a matrix "retains"—think linear independence!
💡 Pro Tip: Use Gaussian elimination to find both the rank and inverse (when possible) in a single process.

📝 Practice Problem

Q: What is the inverse of \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \] ?

Solution:

  • \( \det(A) = 2 \cdot 4 - 3 \cdot 1 = 8 - 3 = 5 \)
  • \( A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \)
