📘 Chapter 2: Inverse and Rank of a Matrix

This chapter focuses on two key properties in linear algebra: Matrix Inverse and Rank. These concepts are essential for solving systems of equations, understanding linear transformations, and working with vector spaces.

🔁 Inverse of a Matrix

  • Only defined for square, non-singular matrices.
  • If \( A^{-1} \) exists, then: \[ A \cdot A^{-1} = A^{-1} \cdot A = I \]
  • The inverse is unique.

Example:

Let \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \] Then \[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{(1)(4)-(2)(3)} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1.5 & -0.5 \end{bmatrix} \]

📏 Rank of a Matrix

  • Rank is the maximum number of linearly independent rows or columns.
  • Rank represents the dimension of the row space or column space.

To Find Rank:

  • Use row-reduction (Gaussian elimination) to get the matrix in echelon form.
  • Count the number of non-zero rows.

Example:

Let \[ B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \] Since Row 2 = 2 × Row 1 ⇒ rank = 1

💡 Key Properties

  • If \( A^{-1} \) exists ⇒ \( \det(A) \neq 0 \) and \( \text{Rank}(A) = n \)
  • If \( \text{Rank}(A) < n \) ⇒ \( A \) is not invertible
  • Every invertible matrix has full rank
Tip 💡: A matrix can have an inverse only when it's square and of full rank. Use row operations to test rank quickly!

📝 Practice Question

Q: What is the inverse of \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \] ?

Solution: First, find the determinant:

\( \det = (2)(4) - (3)(1) = 8 - 3 = 5 \)

Use the formula: \[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \]
